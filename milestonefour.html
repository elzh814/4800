Part B. Testing Protocol

Testing

  • Research Question
    Does our software increase the learning and satisfaction of hybrid learning groups above the most commonly used software? Do hybrid students using our software receive the same level of education as in- person learning groups?
  • Participants
    • Demographic of interest is college students
    • Recruit via on-campus flyers and emails
    • Small monetary reward ($10 per visit, or $80 overall)
  • Informed consent
    • Must disclose purpose of the study, study duration, and methods being used
    • Participant must sign consent form to participate
  • COVID-19 Procedure
    • To conduct this study, we will require masks for the in-person groups and screen for vaccine status for in-person students

Procedure

  • Participants are grouped into one of three possible conditions. Each condition is made of of groups of 4 students.
    • 3 conditions
      1. Hybrid learning group using our software (experimental group)
      2. Hybrid learning group using Zoom for video and audio, and each groupâ€™s choice of note sharing technology (Google docs, github, figma, hard copies, etc) (control 1)
      3. In-person learning group (control 2)
  • Students attend an 8 day course (4 weeks, 2 days a week) about a certain topic
    • We chose a curriculum about human-computer interaction
  • Every group attends class based on the condition they were assigned
    • 2 individuals in hybrid groups are assigned as online students, and 2 are assigned as in-person
  • The second day every week contains a group assignment, with individual final exams on the last day
  • Administer questionnaire with a rating scale about user experience
    • How satisfied were you?
    • How easy was the software to use?
    • How would you rate your learning?
    • Etc.
    • Rating scale 0-4 with 4 being highest score (e.g. very easy to use) and 0 being low score (e.g. very hard to use)
  • Small reward of $10 per day to encourage attendance

Analysis

  • Results come in the form of grades on group assignments and individual exam, as well as satisfaction rating from questionnaire
  • Comparing results between groups
    • Compare mean of project scores from experimental group to means of the control groups
    • Compare mean exam score of experimental group to mean exam scores from controls
    • Comparing mean satisfaction scores of experimental group against controls
  • Comparing results across groups
    • Exam scores from In person students vs online students in hybrid groups (answers the question: do the scores from in-person students skew the group data?)
    • Satisfaction ratings from in-person vs online students (does the software benefit both parties?)
    • Scores and satisfaction ratings: Virtual students (experimental group) vs virtual students (control) (how does our software compare to the alternatives?)

Possible Results

  • If scores are high in between group analysis, we can conclude that our software improved the learning of groups over the alternative softwares
    • Results close to the baseline control (in-person groups condition) we can conclude that our software is as effective as in-person groups
  • Across Groups
    • If scores between in-person and hybrid students are similar, we can rule out the in-person scores as confounds that pull the hybrid groups up
    • If the satisfaction rating are high in both in-person and virtual students from experimental group, we can say the software benefits both parties
    • If scores and satisfaction ratings from the experimental virtual students are better than controls, we can be confident saying our software works better than the alternatives
